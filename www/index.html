Our Pedagogy

The task of education is to learn to interpret the world, and the data revolution of the last ten years rests on new approaches to computer algorithms for automating interpretation. We teach our students that this isn’t magic, and that students from all backgrounds and majors can learn how to use these new approaches to make a better world.

MEANING

Instead of memorizing the rules of proper thinking, students learn to see meaningful patterns emerge from out of the noise. Experts are people who know how to use the tools to interpret the world.

MENTORS

Faculty - like Mentor himself in Homer's Odyssey - point to what is meaningful in the world, and lay out the paths students must decide between. We demand a lot of thought and time because the projects matter.

STUDENTS

Run the show - as a group, and with mentors from the faculty and community. Students learn how to use the tools we make available, and how to visualize projects in terms of data, but the direction they is their choice.

Volunteer Management App

Groups shouldn't just reflect your identity; they should help you do more with others. With our volunteer management apps, users organize groups to get things done inside their organization and establish multiple paths for communicating, assigning responsibilities, and tracking progress. Groups are flexible and overlapping, and the interface is simple and responsive, helping everyone coordinate on shared projects without having to sort through a layer of bureaucracy.

Our first projects were all designed to create the infrastructure for future projects, and the volunteer management apps have given us both the conceptual framework and the actual technology for our next round of projects. We want to help society move, to unleash the potential of technology to help us think, plan and act together toward a better future.

The BonnerBuzz

DASH and the Bonner Leaders are both new in the Honors College, and we share a common commitment to Houston and to service. By providing new tools, our students can contribute to their partner organizations earlier and with greater effect. The BonnerBuzz will let the students track their hours and their skills development, but also organize their teams and collaborate in simple, flexible and - ultimately - powerful ways.

SHAPE Community Center

The SHAPE Community Center has been working in Houston since 1969 to provide community strength and empowerment through a broad range of programs, courses and events that bring together the African American Community of Houston. They'll be using the tool to help track volunteers at projects and get the word out about opportunities.

Community Health Workers Continuing Credit Courses

Community Health Workers are trusted members of a community that serve as navigators through the complexities of our health care system and bridge the gaps between people's needs and the available resources. There are almost a thousand Community Health Workers registered in the greater Houston area, and we are providing a tool to help them see course offerings across the region and track their hours and progress toward their educational goals.

Healthy Homes

We have just started a new collaboration with the UH School of Art and the Menil Museum (with more partners coming soon!) to use high quality hand-held scanners to begin a long term digital humanities and image processing project. Our first scans will be of objects of extraordinary art historical value that cannot be easily moved. We can do the scans in situ and bring them to life online. Then we will work with image processing specialists to explore the new opportunities for digital research in art history.

Arctic Art

Next fall, the Menil Museum will show art from the Ted Carpenter Collection of Arctic Art. This collection includes many pieces of carved walrus ivory that are too fragile to be handled and difficult to see under glass. Scanning will make them available to the curator and the public in new and more easily manipulable form - in 3d and at millimeter scale.

Djenne Terracottas

Many of these beautiful pieces of art from present day Mali were plundered from archeological sites and sold on the black market. Some of the art was glued together from multiple broken statues, and some was simply forged. Through advanced image processing techniques, we will be able to add to the understanding of these works - helping to distinguish originals from fakes and exploring new approaches to categorizing common styles and other clues about the original context of the individual works of art.

Conservation

One of the most difficult conservation tasks at any museum is sculpture made from new materials. Plastic and vinyl, for example, slowly change shape over time. Laser scans will give us information about the structure that is lost in even the best photography, and will allow long-term conservation and care for sculpture in new materials.

Healthy Homes

Looking at your home

The revolution in data is not just about how you process facts, but how you communicate to users what the important facts are. A well-designed inspection tool lets you see your house as a place with strengths and weaknesses - and lets you prioritize your responses. We give users the tools to draw floorplans, add notes and photos, and then compare their data with others.

Healthy Community Inspections

Using the same interface for drawing and adding notes, we also let users gather data about their neighborhoods - understanding how data can be layered at different levels to create new insight.

New avenues for epidemiology

Indoor air pollution has been one of the hardest parts of air pollution exposure for epidemiologists to model. We have one of the best monitor systems in the world for outdoor air, but without a better picture of indoor air we'll never know the full story. As we build data about Houston from the ground up, and from indoors to out, we can build a bridge to new science and to better health.

Clean Air

Collaboration

We're working with UH's Department of Earth and Atmospheric Science on a variety of projects to make Houston's air pollution less of a problem. From real time maps and forecasts of pollution on mobile phones to streaming analytic tools to help sensitive groups understand their risks, and innovative machine learning techniques for epidemiology, we're approaching the whole air as an object of inquiry and a basic condition of health.

We have already produced innovative real time ozone maps with the Houston Clean Air Network, that allow you to see ozone clouds as the form and move over the Houston area. They keep people with air pollution sensitivity from staying indoors all day long when there's an ozone warning - the clouds often pass like an afternoon shower, after all, and you can use this map just like a weather map to see what's coming.

More pollutants

We are working to provide more information about the full mix of air in Houston, including air toxics as well as other sources of smog.

Mold and Pollen

Current readings of mold and pollen are generated using the same technology developed fifty years ago - and we are in a position to change that with innovative new techniques for automated recognition and processing as well as integrated modeling of the spread of pollen throughout the area.

Epidemiology

Working with collaborators at Texas Children's Hospital and Rice, we are exploring new machine learning techniques to old questions about air pollution and health. The problems of multiple pollutants have been especially tricky, and our techniques are particularly promising.

Courses

Open Data: Tools for a New Age

Spring 2015/Peggy Lindner

Conceptual and practical overview for new data analytics tools used in health, environment, business and science. We will learn how to visualize multidimensional data, compare different types of data, and use different types of spatial algorithms for decisions and recommendations across affinity groups. We will be using examples from environmental and social problems facing the city, and expect everyone to get their hands dirty with some light programming.

Philosophy Beyond Production (1): Pollution and Policy

Spring 2015/Dan Price

Humanities courses often talk about how meaning is produced in a work of art, and social science courses teach us about how different cultures produce meaning in different ways. But what do we mean by “producing meaning”? The model is industrial and commercial and has begun to seem quaint in light of modern technology and the new economy. In this course we will use the concepts of a post-industrial economy – data mining, social networks, crowd-sourcing and open data – to understand how meaning occurs in post-industrial culture. We have a very active example (air pollution, regulations, and individual health), and will look at the philosophyof meaning-making in/as culture and work toward a better post-industrial understanding. Readings will begin with important older texts from critical literary and social studies – Foucault, Derrida, Latour and Butler – but quickly move into recent suggestions for a new idea of culture, with a new vocabulary about what matters in a world of data flows and emergent forms.

Team

Dr. Daniel Price

Research Assistant Professor

Dan is a native Houstonian who graduated with a B.A. from Rice in 1987, then spent time living abroad until returning to the U.S. His writing is concerned with the intersection of social, political and aesthetic thought in French and German thinkers and includes Touching Difficulty: Sacred Form from Plato to Derrida (Davies Publishers, 2011). He has taught in various capacities at UH since 2000, but has been increasingly devoted to funded interdisciplinary research projects in the digital humanities and air pollution.

Dr. Peggy Lindner

Research Assistant Professor

Born in East Germany, Peggy has a keen appreciation for the importance of making data free and accessible to everyone. Her training in high performance computing has infused all the projects with higher expectations for quality and stability and her expertise in data representation has made it possible to think about larger and more integrated data projects. In addition to teaching with DASH, she is the liason for digital humanities with the Center for Advanced Computing and Data Sciences here at UH.
